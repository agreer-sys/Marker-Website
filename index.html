Why this exists

The room as a system.

I've been a personal trainer for 30 years. In a small building gym with two or three other trainers, I started paying attention to their programming. Good trainers don't write new workouts every week — they run progressive blocks of the same workout for 4 to 12 weeks at a time. Which means if you watched a trainer-client pair for a few sessions, you could predict exactly what they were going to do next, in what order, on what equipment.

So I learned the room. I knew which client was about to finish at the squat rack, who needed the cables in ten minutes, whose leg day wouldn't touch upper-body equipment for another forty minutes. Because I work in blocks — modular pieces of a workout that can be rearranged without breaking the session — I could route my client around everyone else without compromising their training.

We all got more done. The room moved as a system instead of three pairs competing for the same piece of equipment.

What I was running in my head, for three clients, in one small gym — Marker runs on a map, for every user, in every gym.

How it works

Built at the gym, not before it.

Every other app gives you a workout before you arrive. Marker starts when you walk in the door.

01
Read the room
Marker always knows the plan, and suggests a starting point — where you are in your week, what your split calls for. You confirm what's actually free. The system steers; you decide.
02
Build the block
Marker generates a structured superset from what's available. Pairing respects proximity — no crossing the floor between sets. Your history and goals feed every decision.
03
Train. Move on.
Strict timing. Voice cues. Real rest periods. When the block ends, Marker suggests two or three next equipment options — the ideal next move for your program, given what's available. Every block is part of the same workout. The program holds; the path adapts.
The map

Roads existed before Waze. People drove on them. But nobody had a real-time model of where the cars actually were. Gyms are the same. Marker is building that layer.

Once it exists, every training product becomes possible. Choreographed gym floors. Real-time crowd routing. Equipment analytics for operators. A vision model trained on gym environments before the ambient hardware that uses it fully exists. Every user who trains in an unmapped gym adds to the map. The dataset is the long-term asset.

The first marker

Reps in Reserve. Not rep targets.

Fixed rep counts ask the wrong question. Marker asks the right one.

Stop a set when you have 1–2 reps left. Not when you've hit a number.

Most apps say: "do 4 sets of 10–12." You pick a weight, grind to 12, tick the box. The set was either too easy or too brutal — and the app records the same thing either way.

Marker uses Reps in Reserve (RIR). Pick a weight. Train until you have 1–2 reps left. Log what you got. Research is consistent: 1–2 RIR produces the same hypertrophy as training to failure, with lower fatigue and better recovery across the week.

RIR is the first marker the system reads on every user, every set. Over time, Marker understands your strength relative to a given load better than a rep count ever could. The 70lb EZ bar is taken — you grab the 60. Log what you got. Next session, the system knows.

You don't beat a marker. You meet it.

The longer arc

The name was chosen on purpose.

A marker, in medicine, is an indicator. ApoB. HRV. Fasting glucose. The same move that makes RIR work in training — measure against a reference, adjust, repeat, don't grind to failure — is what serious longevity practice is built on. Marker's arc is to become the operating layer for every marker that matters for healthspan.
